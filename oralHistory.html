The Matthews

Ardelle and Robert Matthews experienced a long and distinguished career in the education field as teachers and administrators. They continue to make significant contributions to the San Diego African American community with their services to many organizations, including the Jackie Robinson YMCA Board, RADY Children's Hospital Auxiliary and Kids New Day, Jacobs Center Committee of Investments, Jack and Jill, Common Ground Theatre, Women Inc., Tema Ghana Sister City, and the Elementary Institute of Science.

Mr. Carell Reavis

Among the first 20,000 African American men who enlisted in the Marines, Dr. Reavis was sent to the segregated Montford Point Camp located in Camp Lejeune, North Carolina. After training, he served in World War II, the Korean War, and Vietnam. Retiring after 21 years in the Corps, he and his family settled in San Diego, he operated a barbershop & sold real estate for 37 years. In 2012, he was awarded the Congressional Gold Medal, the nation's highest civilian honor, "for outstanding perseverance and courage that inspired social change in the Marine Corps."

Dr. Carrol Waymon

Appointed head of San Diego's first human relations agency, Dr. Carrol Waymon, was charged with educating the public about racial tension and identifying racial injustice through community dialogue. Dr. Waymon worked tirelessly to achieve equality for all San Diegans. A retired educator, he is one of the pioneers in establishing what is now known as the Africana Studies department at San Diego State University.

Dr. Dorothy Smith

Dr. Dorothy L W Smith was the first African-American woman to server on the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education. She continued to champion the cause for all students. She has held leadership roles in many organizations including San Diego State University, San Diego City College and The National School Board Association. Dr. Dorothy Smith is also active in the arts and is the secretary of the board of directors for Common Ground Theatre.

Mr. Willie O Ree

Mr. O'Ree was born in Fredericton, New Brunswick. He became the first black player in the National Hockey League on January 19, 1958 playing for the Boston Bruins against the Montreal Canadians. Mr. O'Ree played seven seasons for the San Diego Gulls. After his professional career, he worked as the Director Youth Development and Hockey Ambassador for the National Hockey League. He introduced young boy and girls, many from disadvantaged neighbourhoods into the sport of hockey.

Mr. Edward Fletcher

Edward S. Fletcher retired as San Diego City Schools Director Health Services in 1991, following a career of thirty-six years as a public schools educator. Throughout his career in education Ed remained active in the Marine Corps Reserve and retired in 1991, having attained the rank of Colonel. In 1993-94 he was Interim Executive Director of the San Diego Community Foundation. In 1995-96 he was the Education Director of the San Diego Urban League.
